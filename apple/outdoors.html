Act Like An Animal
(Ages 2+)

All that's needed is imagination.

  • Choose an animal to act out throughout the day, such as during transitions or during play time.
  • Start by suggesting an animal each day or for each transition and engage children by using their suggestions.
  • Adults should play too!

Easy Catching
(Ages 2+)

Make learning to catch easier by gently swinging a suspended ball into a child's arms. All you need is a balloon and a pair of pantyhose.

  • Blow up the balloon until it fits snugly in the large part of the pantyhose.
  • Tie off the top of the hose to prevent balloon pieces from escaping if the balloon pops.
  • Hold the two toes of the hose in one hand so the balloon is off the floor.
  • Gently swing the balloon toward the child until s/he grasps it. Or suspend the hose from a tree branch or doorknob so the child can push the balloon and catch it as it returns.
  • For toddlers, lower the ball slowly into the child's outstretched arms until s/he becomes familiar with grasping the balloon.

Shadow Tag
(Ages 3+)

No special equipment needed — just an open space to run and some sunshine.

  • Have children pair up.
  • Designate one child as the "walker" and the other as the "tagger."
  • Ask the children to find their shadow.
  • On "Go", the tagger tries to tag the "walker" by stepping on his shadow.
  • Have the children switch roles and continue playing.
  • Adults should join the fun!

Let's Stick Together
(Ages 3+)

Children follow a partner and copy their movements.

  • Scatter children in pairs throughout an activity area.
  • Designate one partner as leader and the other as follower.
  • On "Go" followers stick like glue to their partner, moving closely behind them without touching. Try using music and start it on "Go."
  • Prompt leaders to use a variety of movements (skipping, hopping, sliding, bending, etc.) and challenge followers to match their partner's movements.
  • After 20 seconds, signal leaders to freeze — stop the music if you are using it.
  • Leaders stop and lead the partner in a stretch of their choice for 10 seconds.
  • Reverse roles and repeat. Adults should join in too!

Tug on a Sock
(Infants)

Infants love to move and explore their world.

  • Hold a sock so baby grabs it. Pull lightly on the sock so baby tightens his grip.
  • Stop pulling and let baby explore the sock.
  • When baby drops the sock, pick it up and hold it until baby grabs it again.

Early experiences develop muscle strength and endurance needed to enjoy physical activity as they grow older. It's never too early to start.
