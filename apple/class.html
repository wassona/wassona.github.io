Morning Greetings

Use Active Songs to start the day — making up your own tune or rhythm. Help children of all ages "get their wiggles out" and be ready to listen and follow directions.

  • Example: Play the Alphabet Name Game calling each child and caregiver by name:
  • Good morning (name). (name) starts with (first letter of name). Let's use our bodies to make that letter.
  • Adults should play too!

Younger children will catch on with practice. Find more ideas in the Healthy Apple Physical Activity Toolkit.

Circle Time

Circle time becomes 15-20 minutes of structured activity with a few simple props and ideas, such as a Movement Jar.

  • Print a list of exercises, games, movements. Cut them up and put them in a jar — jump like a kangaroo, balance on one foot, and so on.
  • Children take turns picking from the jar for the activity the group will do next.
  • Adults should play, too.

Align the activity with concepts you are working on like counting ("hop 5 times") or days of the week (use a different activity for each day of the week).

Meal/Snack Time

Meal/snack time is a great opportunity to make connections between healthy foods and healthy bodies. It's a chance to incorporate physical activity. Motivate children to eat foods that will make them jump higher, grow taller and run faster.

Try this idea:

  • A provider identifies each of the items (or ingredients) in the meal/snack.
  • Kids stand up each time foods of a certain color/food group/starting letter are called.

Transitions

Take advantage of the transition of changing from one activity to the next by incorporating more movement — like directing the children to follow a leader's movements, act like animals or do exercises (jump, hop, skip, arm circles). Tailor the activity to the age group.

  • For younger children, assign a specific song or movement for each type of transition to help them learn a new routine (example: walk like a monkey).
  • Older children love more surprises with their structure (example: they may know that they act like an animal each time they go outside but won't know which one until the provider calls it out).

Nap Time

Before and after nap time is time for slow movement — to develop balance and stretch muscles to prepare for the transition to/from rest.

  • Put on calm music and join the kids in doing slow stretches or walks to their cots/blankets.
  • Have them lay down in slow motion.

Building on Established Daily Activities

Discover ideas for small, easy ways to include more physical activity into your busy daily routine — ideas that don't disrupt your schedule, but build on established activities, enabling you to take advantage of small moments to get children moving. Each day and each week, those small moments add up.
